Choosing the Right Rifle Calibre for South African Game

Hunting April 2026 6 min read

The .308 vs .30-06 vs .270 debate has kept hunting camps arguing since your oupa's time — and it will never truly be settled, because the honest answer is: it depends on what you hunt, where you hunt, and how much you practise. Here's how we help customers cut through the noise.

Start with the game, not the calibre

Match the cartridge to the animal you'll hunt most often, not the biggest animal you might hunt once in your life.

Small plains game — springbok, blesbok, impala, fallow deer

The classic choice is the .243 Winchester: flat-shooting, mild recoil, and deadly with a good 90–100 gr bullet. The 6.5 Creedmoor has become hugely popular here too — modern, efficient, accurate, and pleasant to shoot, with excellent factory ammo choices. Either will handle Karoo springbok at sensible ranges all day.

Medium game — kudu, gemsbok, red hartebeest, black wildebeest

This is the heartland of the great all-rounders:

  • .270 Winchester — flat trajectory, proven on kudu for nearly a century. A superb "one rifle for the Cape" choice.
  • .308 Winchester — short action, inherently accurate, huge ammo availability, and enough bullet weight (150–180 gr) for anything on this list.
  • .30-06 Springfield — everything the .308 does with a touch more velocity and heavier bullet options. Recoil is a little stiffer.
  • 7×64 / 7mm-08 — beloved by many SA hunters for the excellent sectional density of 7 mm bullets with moderate recoil.

If you'll only ever own one hunting rifle, pick from this group and you will never feel under-gunned on plains game.

Large game and long shots — eland, longer Karoo ranges

The 7mm Rem Mag and .300 Win Mag earn their keep when shots stretch past 250 m or when eland-sized body mass is on the menu. The price is recoil, muzzle blast and more expensive ammunition — which means most shooters practise less with them. Be honest with yourself about that trade-off.

Dangerous game

Buffalo and other thick-skinned dangerous game are a different world entirely — legally and practically. A minimum of .375 H&H Magnum is the accepted (and in most provinces, legislated) standard. That's a specialised purchase; come talk to us before going down this road.

The three questions that actually decide it

  1. Can you shoot it well? Recoil you flinch from costs more game than any ballistic shortfall. A .243 you shoot confidently beats a magnum you fear.
  2. Can you buy ammo for it anywhere? .308, .30-06, .270 and .243 are on the shelf of every dealer in the country — including ours. Exotic calibres can leave you stranded before a hunt.
  3. What does your hunting actually look like? Bushveld shots average 80–150 m; open Karoo can stretch to 250 m+. Buy for your reality, not for YouTube.

Our honest recommendation

For a first and only hunting rifle in the Western Cape: a .270 Win, .308 Win or 6.5 Creedmoor in a solid bolt-action, topped with the best scope you can afford, and at least 60 rounds through it on the range before your first hunt. That combination has accounted for more South African venison than every magnum combined.
