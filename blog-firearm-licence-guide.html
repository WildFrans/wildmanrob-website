How to Get Your First Firearm Licence in South Africa

Hunting May 2026 6 min read

Getting your first firearm licence under the Firearms Control Act (FCA) feels intimidating — but it's a well-worn path, and thousands of South Africans walk it every year. Broken into steps, it's completely manageable. Even better: we assist with the whole process in-store.

Step 1: Pass your competency training

Before SAPS will consider you competent, you must complete training with an accredited training provider covering the legal side (the FCA itself) and safe handling of your firearm type — handgun, rifle or shotgun. You'll do a theory exam and practical assessment. Most people comfortably complete it over a weekend or two. We can put you in touch with trusted local training providers.

Step 2: Apply for your competency certificate

With your training certificates in hand, you apply at your local SAPS station's Designated Firearms Officer (DFO). You'll need your ID, training proficiency certificates, photographs and two references, among other paperwork. SAPS runs background checks — criminal record, violence history, and they may interview people close to you. Be patient: this stage can take several months.

Step 3: Choose your firearm and licence type

You apply for a licence for a specific firearm, so this is when you buy (the dealer holds it in stock until your licence is issued). The licence section matters:

  • Section 13 — self-defence: one firearm (typically a handgun or shotgun).
  • Section 15 — occasional hunting & sport shooting: for the hunter who goes out a few times a year.
  • Section 16 — dedicated hunting & sport shooting: for members with dedicated status through an accredited association (like NARFO — which we can register you with in-store). Allows more firearms and longer validity.

Your motivation letter is the heart of the application: it explains why you need this firearm for this purpose. A weak motivation is the number one cause of refusals — and it's exactly where our experience helps most.

Step 4: Submit and wait (properly prepared)

The licence application goes through your DFO with your competency certificate, motivation, and proof of a compliant safe (yes — you need an SABS-approved safe installed before you apply; read our safe storage guide). Processing times vary from a few months to considerably longer. Track your application status and keep copies of everything.

Step 5: Collect your licence and firearm

Once approved, your licence card is issued, and you collect your firearm from the dealer. We'll hand it over with a full safety briefing, help you choose ammunition, and get you booked for range time so your first shots are supervised and confident.

Common mistakes that sink applications

  • Generic, copy-paste motivation letters
  • Buying a safe after the inspection instead of before
  • Expired training certificates or missing documents
  • Choosing the wrong licence section for how you'll actually use the firearm
