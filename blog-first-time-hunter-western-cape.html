5 Things Every First-Time Hunter in the Western Cape Should Know

Hunting May 2026 5 min read

Your first hunt is something you'll remember for the rest of your life — and a little preparation makes the difference between a memorable weekend and an expensive lesson. Here's what our team at Wildman Robertson tells every new hunter who walks through our doors.

1. Sort your paperwork out early — much earlier than you think

You cannot legally hunt with a firearm you don't hold a licence for. If you're starting from zero, budget serious lead time: you'll first need a competency certificate (which requires accredited training and a SAPS application) and then your firearm licence itself. SAPS processing times vary widely, so if you're dreaming of hunting this winter season, the paperwork should have started months ago.

The good news: you don't have to own a rifle to hunt. Many game farms hire out rifles for the duration of your hunt under supervision, and hunting with a borrowed firearm under the direct supervision of the licensed owner is a common way for new hunters to get started legally while their own licence is in process. Come chat to us — we handle licensing assistance in-store and can map out the fastest legal route for your situation.

2. Hunting season in the Cape is short — plan around it

Most plains game hunting in South Africa happens in the cooler winter months, roughly May to August, when the veld is dry, the grass is low and meat handles well. Popular Western Cape and Karoo species — springbok, blesbok, kudu, gemsbok, fallow deer — are all typically hunted in this window. Farms book out fast, especially for long weekends, so lock in your dates early and confirm exactly which species your outfitter's permits and exemptions cover.

3. Shot placement beats calibre — every time

New hunters agonise over calibre choice, but ask any experienced Karoo hunter and they'll tell you: a well-placed shot from a .243 beats a poor shot from a .300 magnum. Spend your preparation time on the shooting range, not the forums. Practise from field positions — off shooting sticks, sitting, and prone — not just off a bench. If you can consistently put three shots into a saucer at 100 m from sticks, you're ready for most Cape plains game distances.

We have a full range of calibres in stock and can talk you through what suits the game you're after — read our calibre guide for a head start.

4. Dress for the Karoo, not the catalogue

Western Cape winter hunting means freezing dawns and warm middays — often in the same morning. Layers are everything: a warm beanie and gloves for the pre-dawn drive, a fleece or softshell you can shed by 10 am, and tough, quiet trousers for walking through scrub. Good boots that are already walked in are non-negotiable. You don't need head-to-toe camo for plains game; neutral earth tones work perfectly well.

5. Respect the animal — from shot to plate

Ethical hunting doesn't end when the trigger breaks. Know your limits and don't take marginal shots. Once your animal is down, the work starts: bleeding, gutting and cooling the carcass quickly protects the venison you've just earned. Most farms will help with slaughter and processing, but bring cooler boxes — biltong, droëwors and a freezer full of venison are the real trophies of a first hunt.
